WHAT THIS COMPARISON PROVES
1
Same pipeline, different stopping points
Both transactions went through identical engines in the same order. The difference wasn't the pipeline — it was the verdicts. Side A got green at every step. Side B accumulated risk signals (NRD flag, low reputation, unknown hash, executable) that the ML layer recognised as collectively suspicious, triggering escalation.
2
Risk signals compound
No single signal on Side B was enough to block. Newly registered domain — not enough. Unknown hash — not enough. Executable file — not enough. But combined, ML flagged it as suspicious. This is exactly why Pillar 5 exists: to catch zero-days that no individual signature can catch.
3
Sandbox is the last resort
Side A never went near the sandbox — no suspicious file, no need. Side B only reached the sandbox after ML escalated and pre-filter couldn't verdict cheaply. This is the early-termination design principle in reverse: expensive engines only run when cheaper ones couldn't decide.
4
Cloud Effect makes the network learn
Side B's finding was published globally the moment the sandbox verdict landed. The next customer on Earth to encounter that file gets blocked at Pillar 2 — without the sandbox ever running again. Every detection makes the platform smarter for everyone.
⟳
F1 PIT-LANE ANALOGY
Both cars (Side A, Side B) drive through the same pit lane. Same crew, same inspection stations, same sequence. Side A's car looks fine to every crew member — lollipop stays up, car keeps moving, pits out in milliseconds. Side B's car raises small concerns at several stations — none fatal on their own — but the telemetry crew (ML) notices the pattern and radios the engineering bay (sandbox) to take the suspect component apart. The engineers find it's faulty, and the team broadcasts the finding to every other team in the sport (Cloud Effect). Next race, every car with that part gets flagged immediately — no engineering-bay time needed.
→
EXAM TIP
If a scenario question gives you multiple risk signals (NRD + unknown hash + executable + low reputation) and asks which engine catches the threat — the answer is almost always ATP Pillar 5 (ML / Page Risk) triggering sandbox escalation. Individual signals pass earlier engines; ML is the one that combines them.

MENTAL NOTE
DLP is shown as "N/A" on both sides because both are GET requests — nothing meaningful is being uploaded. DLP's real action happens on POST, PUT, and file uploads. Don't expect DLP to catch a malicious download.
